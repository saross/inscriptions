# Scout 2 — Urban scaling law on Roman inscriptions / material culture

## Executive summary

The Ortman-Bettencourt framework has been applied to Roman material culture extensively since 2017, but **inscriptions have never been used as the primary scaling variable in a formal population-output power-law regression** of the type Y = Y₀N^β. The closest parallel is Hanson (2021), which establishes a consistent but **sublinear** relationship between inscription counts and estimated site population, and explicitly frames inscriptions as analogous to information infrastructure rather than a direct socioeconomic output. Carleton et al. 2025 (*Nature Cities*) uses inscription counts as one of several proxies for elite wealth concentration, also with sublinear results, within a Bayesian framework. **Critically, no published study has reported a super-linear (β > 1) scaling exponent for Latin inscriptions vs population.** This substantially changes the character of the scaling-law concern for the current project.

On reporting convention, the Bettencourt-Lobo-Ortman tradition leads with **raw power-law fits** and presents residuals as secondary diagnostics; per-capita or residual-first approaches are methodologically advocated but less commonly executed in the archaeological branch. This gap is consequential: there is no established norm in Roman scaling archaeology for choosing one framing over the other when reporting secondary correlations.

## Reporting convention finding

The Bettencourt-Lobo-Ortman programme **argues methodologically** that scaling residuals (SAMIs) are more informative than raw or per-capita metrics because per-capita conflates general urbanisation effects with local dynamics (Bettencourt et al. 2010, 2015).

But in **Roman archaeological practice**:
- Hanson-Ortman 2019, 2020, Ortman-Lobo 2024 all **lead with raw power-law fits** and present residuals as secondary diagnostics.
- Hanson-Ortman-Lobo 2017 does not compute residuals at all.
- Carleton et al. 2025 uses Bayesian model with population uncertainty, presents β + credible interval as primary.
- **No Roman scaling paper computes scaling residuals for inscriptions and uses them as dependent variable in a secondary correlation.**

No established convention in this sub-literature for framing a correlation of a third variable with inscription counts when both are potentially affected by population size.

## Critical implications for the current paper

### FLAG 1 (critical)
Hanson 2021 reports **sublinear** scaling of inscriptions with population — **the opposite of what my Q2 brief assumed (super-linear per Ortman default).** This materially changes the scaling-law framing:

- **Sublinear β < 1** means inscriptions grow slower than population → per-capita inscription rates **decrease** with city size.
- A raw SPA-inscription correlation does **not** conflate "population effect" with "scaling effect" in the way it would for super-linear variables. If anything, the population signal is partially suppressed relative to a super-linear expectation.
- The conservative case (Framing A: residuals-primary) is **weaker** than I argued. **Framing B (raw correlation primary, scaling as sensitivity) is now the better-supported choice.**
- Hanson's "inscriptions as information infrastructure" framing aligns with sublinear scaling — worth citing as the theoretical frame that makes the sublinearity interpretable.

### FLAG 2 (critical)
**No published study has applied scaling-law analysis to SPA scores or mixture-corrected inscription type distributions.** This paper is the first to do so. The framing choice is entirely methodologically motivated with no community norm — strengthens the case for explicit theoretical justification in the paper.

### FLAG 3
**Carleton et al. 2025 is directly competitive/adjacent.** Check supplementary materials for specific β posterior distributions for inscription counts — these would be the best available Bayesian prior for the project's analysis. Code at [github.com/wccarleton/urbanscale](https://github.com/wccarleton/urbanscale) (CC BY 4.0).

### FLAG 4
**Ortman, Lobo et al. 2024 is the best methodological parallel.** Uses raw power-law regression of material output vs settlement population, with binned means and White-corrected standard errors. If the project also uses building count or excavated area alongside Hanson estimates, the 2024 methodology section transfers directly.

## Build-vs-adopt verdict

**Cite, don't adopt.** Hanson 2021's sublinear result should be cited as **prior art establishing that the scaling-law concern is weaker than it would be for a super-linear variable.** The paper will need to run its own scaling regression (SPA-corrected inscription count vs Hanson population) because Hanson 2021 uses different inscription selection and different sites than the project's `urban_context_pop_est` data. The resulting β will be an original contribution.

**Framing B (raw correlation primary) is now well-supported.** Add scaling-residual analysis as robustness check. Cite Hanson 2021 explicitly for the sublinear expectation.

## Critiques to engage

- **Smith 2019, 2023** — heterogeneity / dimensions-of-urbanism critique of SST single-exponent models.
- **Cross-sectional vs longitudinal confound** — measured β in cross-sectional data does not reflect individual-city dynamics.
- **Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP)** — sensitivity to settlement-boundary definitions.
- **Selection bias / survivorship** — small sites under-represented; acute for inscriptions.
- **Tautology risk** — if settled area (used to estimate population) correlates with inscription preservation (urban excavation intensity), testing inscriptions vs population partly tests inscriptions vs excavation effort. **Not directly addressed in the literature — gap this paper could address.**
